Black Hole

Don't let the name fool you: a black hole is anything but empty space!

Rather, it is a great amount of matter packed into a very small area - think of a star ten times more massive than the Sun squeezed into a sphere approximately the diameter of a city.

Black holes are regions of spacetime exhibiting such strong gravitational effects that nothing—not even particles and electromagnetic radiation such as light—can escape from inside it. The theory of general relativity predicts that a sufficiently compact mass can deform spacetime to form a black hole.

The boundary of the region from which no escape is possible is called the event horizon. Although the event horizon has an enormous effect on the fate and circumstances of an object crossing it, no locally detectable features appear to be observed. In many ways, a black hole acts like an ideal black body, as it reflects no light. Moreover, quantum field theory in curved spacetime predicts that event horizons emit Hawking radiation, with the same spectrum as a black body of a temperature inversely proportional to its mass. This temperature is on the order of billionths of a kelvin for black holes of stellar mass, making it essentially impossible to observe.

Artist's depiction of a blazar: a galaxy powered by a supermassive black hole, which produces powerful jets of matter pointing directly toward Earth. Blazars are among the brightest objects in the cosmos in gamma rays.

Artist's depiction of GRO J1655-40, a binary system consisting of a blue star and a stellar-mass black hole. As the black hole pulls matter from the star, it forms the disk and jets shown in the picture, making the black hole bright.

Artist's depiction of a quasar: a supermassive black hole at the center of a galaxy. Matter falling onto the black hole heats up and emits enough radiation to make quasars some of the brightest objects in the universe.

When galaxies collide, their supermassive black holes may interact, sometimes even flinging one of the black holes out of the galaxy. This artistic representation shows a recoiling black hole similar to one discovered in astronomical observations.

An illustration of matter swirling near a supermassive black hole. The flow of matter generated by black holes has a profound influence on their host galaxies, either powering or throttling star formation depending on circumstances.

This artist's illustration depicts the aftermath of a neutron star merger, which is one way black holes might form. Such collisions produce gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) and gravitational waves, providing a way to study black hole births.
